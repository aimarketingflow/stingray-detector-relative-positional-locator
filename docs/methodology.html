üî¨ The Science: RF Echo Location

Our methodology is inspired by echolocation used by bats and dolphins, but applied to radio frequency (RF) signals. Just as bats use sound waves to map their environment, we use RF signals to locate surveillance devices.

üì° How Traditional Echolocation Works

Bats emit sound waves that bounce off objects and return as echoes. By analyzing:

  • Time delay - How long the echo takes to return (distance)
  • Direction - Where the echo comes from (location)
  • Intensity - How strong the echo is (size/proximity)
  • Frequency changes - Doppler effect (movement)

Bats can build a complete 3D map of their surroundings in total darkness.

üìª Our RF Echo Location Adaptation

Instead of emitting signals (which would be illegal), we passively listen to RF signals already being transmitted by Stingrays. By analyzing:

  • Signal strength - How powerful the signal is (distance)
  • Direction - Which way our antenna points for strongest signal (bearing)
  • Multipath reflections - Signals bouncing off buildings (environment mapping)
  • Temporal changes - How signals change over time (movement detection)

We can pinpoint the exact 3D location of a Stingray device without transmitting anything ourselves.

üéØ The 5-Step Detection Methodology

1

Baseline Establishment

Map the "normal" RF environment by scanning all cellular frequencies when no threat is present.

2

Anomaly Detection

Compare current scans to baseline to identify new, unusual, or suspicious signals.

3

Directional Scanning

Point antenna in 8 directions (N, S, E, W, NE, NW, SE, SW, Up, Down) to find signal source.

4

Triangulation

Scan from multiple locations to calculate exact 3D position using signal strength mathematics.

5

Movement Tracking

Monitor over time to determine if device is stationary (fixed) or mobile (vehicle-mounted).

‚ö†Ô∏è Real-World Adjustments

The FSPL formula assumes "free space" (no obstacles). In reality:

  • Buildings cause signal attenuation (weakening)
  • Reflections create multipath interference
  • Weather affects signal propagation
  • Antenna orientation impacts received power

Our toolkit accounts for these factors through multipath analysis and multiple measurement points.

üèóÔ∏è Multipath Analysis: Using Reflections

Just as bats use echoes from multiple surfaces to build a complete picture, we analyze signal reflections from buildings and structures to:

üè¢ Identify Reflectors

By analyzing signal variance and frequency-selective fading, we can detect nearby buildings or structures that are reflecting the signal.

üìè Estimate Distances

The path difference between direct and reflected signals tells us how far the reflector (building) is from the source.

üéØ Improve Accuracy

Multiple reflections from different angles help triangulate the source more precisely, even with obstacles in the way.

üìä Signal Strength Interpretation

Understanding dBm (Decibel-Milliwatts)

Signal strength is measured in dBm, a logarithmic scale where:

  • -10 to -30 dBm: üî¥ VERY STRONG - Device is very close (<100 meters)
  • -30 to -50 dBm: üü° STRONG - Typical cell tower at moderate distance
  • -50 to -70 dBm: üü¢ MODERATE - Normal cellular signal
  • -70 to -90 dBm: üîµ WEAK - Edge of coverage
  • Below -90 dBm: ‚ö´ VERY WEAK - Barely detectable

Key Insight: Stingrays typically show -10 to -30 dBm because they're deployed nearby (in vehicles, on poles, in backpacks) to ensure they capture your phone's signal before legitimate towers do.

üö® Threat Scenarios We Protect Against

üöó Vehicle-Mounted Surveillance

Threat: Hackers or criminals park a vehicle with Stingray equipment outside your home or business.

Detection: Our movement tracking identifies mobile sources that come and go, with signal strength changes indicating the vehicle's proximity.

üè¢ Fixed Installation

Threat: Device mounted on utility poles, rooftops, or hidden in fake equipment boxes for long-term surveillance.

Detection: Persistent signals at the same location over days/weeks, with triangulation pinpointing the exact mounting point.

üéí Portable/Backpack Units

Threat: Compact Stingrays carried in backpacks in crowded areas (protests, events, public spaces).

Detection: Sudden appearance of strong signals in unusual locations, with rapid movement patterns matching pedestrian speed.

üè† Neighbor/Adjacent Property

Threat: Device placed in adjacent building or property to target specific individuals or businesses.

Detection: Directional scanning points to specific building, with multipath analysis confirming the source location.
